## How AI transforms construction procurement

### The technical architecture that matters

An AI-native construction procurement platform should be designed around **multi-agent orchestration with mandatory human-in-the-loop gates for financial transactions**. The most relevant architectural pattern uses **LangGraph** (the leading framework for production agentic AI) to model procurement workflows as directed graphs with persistent state:

1. **Document Ingestion Agent**: OCR + NLP extracts vendor info, line items, amounts, project codes from invoices, POs, and submittals. Construction documents are highly structured and cross-referencing (specs reference drawings, which reference other specs), requiring multimodal capabilities.

2. **Validation Agent**: Performs 3-way matching (invoice ↔ PO ↔ delivery receipt), flags duplicates, detects price outliers against historical data, and checks budget thresholds. Field Materials AI claims **90% reduction** in invoice processing time with this approach.

3. **Compliance Agent**: RAG-powered checks against building codes, contract terms, and regulatory requirements. RAG is the killer application for construction — the industry's massive document corpus (specifications, building codes, RFIs, submittals) is ideal for retrieval-augmented generation. Companies like Ichi AI already reduce 60-120 minute RFI responses to 10-20 minute reviews using this pattern.

4. **Approval Router Agent**: Confidence-based routing — auto-approves routine invoices from trusted vendors below configurable thresholds; escalates high-value, anomalous, or low-confidence items for human review. LangGraph's `interrupt()` function enables clean pause/resume at approval gates.

5. **ERP Integration Agent**: Posts approved transactions to accounting systems with idempotent operations preventing duplicate payments and event sourcing providing complete audit trails.

**Human-in-the-loop is non-negotiable for money movement.** Financial transactions are irreversible or costly to reverse, AI models can hallucinate, and regulatory compliance often mandates human oversight. The design pattern is threshold-based escalation: when AI confidence falls below a configurable threshold (e.g., 0.65), or when transaction amounts exceed limits, workflows pause for human approval. Industry research shows HITL achieves **99.9% accuracy** in document extraction versus 92% for AI-only.

### The money movement architecture (relevant to Rosie)

Designing money movement for construction requires several critical patterns. **Double-entry bookkeeping** ensures every transaction records in two ledger accounts with equal amounts — one debit, one credit — providing end-to-end traceability across GCs, subcontractors, and suppliers. **Idempotency keys** on every payment request prevent duplicate payments (critical when construction payment amounts are large). The **saga pattern** orchestrates multi-step payment flows (GC → subcontractor → supplier) with compensating transactions for rollback on partial failures. **Event sourcing** creates an immutable audit trail of all state changes, and **CQRS** separates write models (payment processing) from read models (dashboards and reporting).

Successful implementations in adjacent industries validate these patterns: Vic.ai processes invoices **5x faster at 80% lower cost**; Landsec achieved **92% time savings** on AP with AI automation; McKinsey reports AI can **reduce procurement costs by 20%** and boost efficiency by 50%.

## Will Larson's frameworks and what they mean for this role

### The engineering leadership mental models

Will Larson's **"An Elegant Puzzle"** provides the vocabulary Source likely uses for engineering organization. His most cited framework describes teams in four states: **Falling Behind** (hire more people), **Treading Water** (reduce WIP), **Repaying Debt** (find time to continue), and **Innovating** (maintain slack). Progress between states is slow, and the key principle is to fix one team at a time rather than spreading hires across teams. For a company at Source's scale (~60-114 employees, engineering team of perhaps 5-15), understanding where teams sit on this spectrum and how to move them forward demonstrates organizational thinking.

His **"Staff Engineer"** framework defines four archetypes: **Tech Lead** (guides execution of one team/cluster, most common), **Architect** (owns direction within a critical domain), **Solver** (digs into knotty problems until resolved), and **Right Hand** (extends executive attention). At Source's size, the Tech Lead and Architect archetypes are most relevant. The critical distinction Larson draws is that **Staff is not "senior but more" — it's a fundamentally different job** based on influence and leadership without authority, focused on organizational impact rather than personal coding output.

Larson's formula for engineering strategy is memorable and practical: *"Write five design documents, pull the similarities out — that's your engineering strategy. Write five engineering strategies, forecast their implications two years out — that's your engineering vision."* Good strategy is **boring** — it emerges from bottom-up organizational learning, not top-down brilliance. Strategy should be opinionated and show its work.

### How to demonstrate Staff-level thinking in interviews

Five keys from the Staff-level system design interview framework stand out:

- **Match communication to the interviewer's level.** Talk like a peer. Use common patterns as shortcuts. Over-explaining signals inexperience.
- **Identify the crux.** For Source, the hard problems are multi-party payment integrity, AI reliability for financial workflows, and scaling procurement operations across diverse project types.
- **Ruthlessly cut complexity.** Staff engineers design simple systems. Start basic, acknowledge deficiencies, solve pinch points as they arise. Ask "does this actually need to scale?" before reaching for distributed solutions.
- **Demonstrate depth through experience.** Pull concrete lessons from past systems. The premium in Staff compensation is your experiential pattern library.
- **Show instincts by making decisions.** "I'm going with Postgres because I need transactions and durability" is stronger than "we could use Postgres or DynamoDB depending on requirements."
